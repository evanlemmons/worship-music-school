# Musicianship Track — Topics

**Format:** ~20 minutes each week. **Philosophy:** teach the things nobody teaches you.

Anyone can learn the notes from YouTube. What separates a kid who can play at home from a musician a worship team actually wants is everything *around* the notes: how sound gets from your hands to the room, how a band communicates, how you show up prepared, and how a set comes together. That's this track. Every topic below pairs a short teach with a **hands-on demo** — because a DI box explained is forgettable, but a DI box you plugged in and heard the difference through is not.

Wherever possible the week's topic connects to that week's song and even the Bible study (structure in music ↔ structure in the Psalms; serving the song ↔ servant not star).

---

## Fall Term — "The Ecosystem of Playing"

### Week 1 — What a band actually is
The roles on a worship team and how a Sunday really comes together: music director/band leader, the rhythm section (drums, bass, keys, rhythm guitar) as the engine, lead/electric, vocals and harmonies, and the tech team behind it all. Set expectations for the group: prepared, on time, humble, having fun.
**Demo:** Walk the room to each instrument; have each leader play the *same* eight bars so students hear what each part contributes to one groove.

### Week 2 — How sound gets to the room (signal flow)
The single most useful mental model in live music: instrument → (DI box or amp/mic) → stage box/snake → console → PA speakers → the room; and a parallel path to monitors/in-ears. Once you can picture the chain, you can troubleshoot almost anything ("where in the chain did it break?").
**Demo:** Trace one real signal path live — plug a bass or acoustic through a DI to the console and literally point at each stage as the sound travels.

### Week 3 — Cables, connections & clean power
XLR vs. 1/4" (TS vs. TRS), why "balanced" signals reject noise on long runs, what a DI box does and when you need one, and clean/consistent power — why we don't daisy-chain into a random wall outlet and why gear likes stable power. Cable care (the over-under wrap) so gear lasts.
**Demo:** Pass around an XLR, a TS instrument cable, and a DI. Do the "hum" demo: a long unbalanced run vs. a balanced run so they *hear* why XLR exists. Teach the over-under cable wrap and have everyone try it.

### Week 4 — In-ear monitors & stage sound
Why modern stages are quiet: in-ears vs. floor wedges, what a personal monitor mix is ("more me"), how to build a usable mix (start with click + your own instrument, add what you need to lock in), and — non-negotiable — protecting your hearing.
**Demo:** Let students listen to a simple in-ear mix and add/remove instruments so they hear what "building a mix" means. Talk decibels and why IEMs at a sane volume protect their ears for a lifetime.

### Week 5 — Tuning, tone & gain staging
Tuning to a shared reference and why an out-of-tune instrument poisons the whole band; the basics of gain staging (set levels so nothing clips and nothing's buried); and a first pass at instrument-appropriate *tone* (bass: clean and present; keys: the right patch; guitar: not too much gain).
**Demo:** Tune every instrument together to the same reference. Then intentionally set a bad gain (too hot, then too quiet) and a good one so they hear the difference.

### Week 6 — The click and the loop
Playing to a metronome/click track; why the click is the band's shared heartbeat; an intro to tracks and pads (the ambient bed a lot of modern worship uses) and how they lock a band together. Timing is the one skill every player is judged on.
**Demo:** Everyone plays a simple groove *without* a click (hear it drift), then *with* a click (hear it lock). Let a student conduct the band starting and stopping on the count.

### Week 7 — Rehearsing at home
How to actually practice so rehearsal isn't where you learn the song: listen to the recording on repeat, learn from a chart, slow the hard part down and loop it, practice *to* the track, and mark your own roadmap. Come to band rehearsal already knowing your part — rehearsal is for playing *together*, not learning notes.
**Demo:** Take that week's song and model a 10-minute home-practice session out loud — including looping one tricky bar and playing along with the recording.

### Week 8 — Charts & the number system
Reading a chord chart, song structure/roadmap markings (intro, V, PC, C, B, tag), and the Nashville Number System so you can transpose a song to any key on the fly — the single skill that makes you a "call any key" musician.
**Demo:** Put that week's song on a chart, then rewrite the chorus in numbers and transpose it to a new key together in real time.

### Week 9 — Rehearsing as a band
What a good rehearsal looks like: start on time, run for arrangement first then details, listen more than you play, and lock the rhythm section (bass + kick drum are married). Dynamics as a team decision, not five soloists.
**Demo:** Run that week's song twice — first everyone plays full the whole time (muddy), then with dynamics and space. Let them hear how "playing less" sounds like more.

### Week 10 — The music director
What an MD/band leader does before and during a service: sets the arrangement and keys, counts things off, and cues the band in real time. Onstage communication — hand signals, eye contact, verbal cues on the mic — and the skill of *following* a leader.
**Demo:** Teach a few standard cues (build, drop/soft, one more time, tag, cutoff) and have a student "MD" the band through a song using only those signals.

### Week 11 — Dynamics & arrangement
Building a song like a story: intro, verses, pre-chorus lift, chorus, breakdown, bridge, big moment, tag. Leaving space; playing for the song, not for yourself; how the same three chords feel different at each stage.
**Demo:** Take one chorus and arrange it three ways — full band, just pad + vocal, and building back up — so students feel how arrangement creates emotion.

### Week 12 — Planning a set & picking songs
How a set is built: flow and momentum, keys that transition well, matching songs to a theme or moment, and why song *order* matters. This is the on-ramp to Planning Center Services.
**Demo:** As a group, build a 3-song set from the songs learned so far — argue about order and keys, then play the transitions.

### Week 13 — The production world
The people and gear that make a service happen beyond the band: front-of-house sound, monitors, lighting, running lyrics/ProPresenter, cameras/stream, and the stage plot & input list that tell everyone where things go. Respecting and communicating with the tech team.
**Demo:** Draw the group's own stage plot and input list on a whiteboard — where each player stands, what plugs in where — the same document a real production team works from.

### Week 14 — Soundcheck & line check (Showcase)
Putting it all together for the Showcase: how to run a line check (confirm every input one at a time), a soundcheck (get levels and monitor mixes), and settle nerves before you play. Then perform.
**Demo:** Run a real line check and soundcheck for the Showcase set. This *is* the lesson.

## Winter Term — "Becoming a Working Band"

### Week 15 — Gear deep-dive I: strings & keys
Guitar and bass signal chains — amps vs. DI, the basics of effects pedals (tuner, overdrive, delay, reverb) and pedal order; keys and pads — patches, splits/layers, and the sound that glues a band. What gear actually earns a spot on your board and what's just GAS (gear acquisition syndrome).
**Demo:** Run a guitar or bass through a couple of pedals and change the order live; show the same keyboard part on three different patches.

### Week 16 — Gear deep-dive II: drums & vocals
Drums — tuning and muffling, acoustic vs. electronic kits, playing to tracks/triggers, and the kick-snare backbone; vocals — mic technique (distance, proximity, not eating the mic), monitors for singers, and blend/harmony basics.
**Demo:** Tune a snare high and low so they hear the difference; do a quick mic-distance demo on a vocalist (too far, too close, right).

### Week 17 — Communication & tools
How real teams communicate and stay organized: Planning Center (schedules, plans, the song's chart/MP3/key all in one place), group chats, and shared charts. Rehearsal etiquette and the underrated skill of *being reliable* — replying, showing up, being prepared.
**Demo:** Walk through a real Planning Center Services plan for a Sunday — show how a scheduled player sees their music, their key, and their spot in the order.

### Week 18 — Being a bandmate
The rhythm-section relationship (bass locks to the kick drum), feel and pocket, listening as a skill, and serving the song *and* each other. The difference between a group of players and an actual band.
**Demo:** Isolate just bass + drums on a groove and dial in the lock; then add the rest of the band on top so they hear the foundation.

### Week 19 — Leading vs. playing
What changes when you're the worship leader, not just a player: engaging and leading a congregation, singing/talking on mic, holding the room, and the servant-leadership posture behind it.
**Demo:** Have a student lead the band and "the congregation" (the rest of the group) through one song, including a spoken cue or transition.

### Week 20 — Building & running a full set
From single songs to a connected set: planning a multi-song run, transitions and segues, key relationships, and keeping momentum across a whole set instead of stopping between every song.
**Demo:** Build and run a full 3–4 song set with real transitions — no stopping between songs.

### Week 21 — Record it & review it
Capturing a rehearsal on a phone or interface, then listening back *critically* but kindly — timing, dynamics, blend — and how to give and receive feedback without egos. Self-review is how pros improve fastest.
**Demo:** Record the band playing one song, then listen back together and each name one thing to keep and one thing to fix.

### Week 22 — Put it together & what's next
A full set run as the "final," a review of how far the band's come, and a real conversation about next steps: serving on a North Metro team, growing a specific skill, or where to plug in this spring.
**Demo:** Full set performance for each other; then map each student's next step.

## Running list of "life of a musician" topics to weave in anytime
Beyond the scheduled topics, keep pulling from these as they come up naturally: how to accept a gig/serving slot professionally, showing up early and tearing down last, stage presence and what to do with your face, dealing with mistakes mid-song (keep going — the click doesn't stop), protecting your hearing for a 40-year career, taking care of your gear and other people's gear, being coachable, the business side (session work, being dependable = getting called back), and the spiritual discipline of not letting skill outrun character.
