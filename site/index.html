The idea

Discipleship, taught through music

Most churches can teach a teenager to play an instrument, and many can run a Bible study. Very few do both in the same room, on purpose, led by people who have done it professionally. This group closes that gap.

We want students to leave able to explain what worship really is and live like it, to understand the whole ecosystem of being a working musician — not just the notes, but the gear, the team, and the preparation — and to sit in with a band and confidently hold their part on a real stage. The name is a working title; the heart of it is discipleship through music.

Every week, the same shape

The three-part night

Ground, sharpen, play — in that order, so that playing together is the celebration the rest of the night points toward.

01

Discipleship

~15 minutes

A short, discussion-driven Bible study (NIV). The first term explores worship as a whole life, not just singing — and we get to know each other.

02

Musicianship

~20 minutes

The craft nobody teaches you: signal flow and gear, in-ears, playing to a click, charts, rehearsing, the music director, planning a set, and production.

03

Playing together

~25 minutes

We learn and work a song as a band and actually play it. Repertoire leans worship, with the occasional secular song for range and fun.

Timing assumes a ~60-minute meeting. If we stretch to 90 minutes, expand to roughly 25 / 30 / 35; for a full two hours, the extra time goes to hands-on gear and a longer band run-through.

Section 01 · the full arc

Discipleship & Bible studies

Translation: NIV · Posture: non-denominational, Christ-centered, discussion-driven Format: ~15 minutes each week — open together, read the passage, talk, close.

The organizing idea for the first two terms is that worship is not a music genre — it's a way of living. Singing is one expression of it; the whole of a surrendered life is the real thing (Romans 12:1). If students grasp that early, everything else — how they practice, how they treat their bandmates, how they carry themselves on and off a stage — flows out of it. The Fall term builds that theology of worship; the Winter term turns it toward the character of a worshiper and a leader.

Each study lists the core passage(s), the big idea, three or four discussion questions, and — where it fits — a one-line tie to that week's musicianship or song. Leaders should feel free to go where the conversation goes; these are rails, not scripts.

Fall Term — "What Is Worship?"

Week 1 (Sep 6) — Kickoff: What is worship, really?

Passage: Romans 12:1–2. Big idea: Worship is offering your whole self to God — "your true and proper worship" — not just what happens when the music plays. Discuss: When you hear the word "worship," what's the first picture in your head? · What would it mean for practicing your instrument, or how you treat people, to be "worship"? · What does it mean to not be "conformed to the pattern of this world"? Tie-in: Sets the theme for the whole group. Great night to also just get to know each other — names, instruments, why they came.

Week 2 (Sep 13) — Worship in spirit and truth

Passage: John 4:1–26 (Jesus and the woman at the well; focus vv. 19–24). Big idea: God isn't after a location or a style — He's after worshipers who come in spirit and in truth. Discuss: The woman wanted to argue about where to worship — what "where/how" debates do we still have? · What might "in spirit and in truth" look like for us? · Why do you think Jesus says the Father is seeking worshipers?

Week 3 (Sep 20) — Made to worship

Passage: Genesis 1:26–31 and Psalm 8. Big idea: We're made in God's image to reflect His glory; worship is us doing what we were built for. Discuss: Psalm 8 asks "what is mankind that you are mindful of them?" — how does it feel that God is mindful of you? · If we're made to reflect God, what are we reflecting when we're not worshiping Him? · Where do you feel most "alive" — and could that be a clue to how you're wired to worship?

Week 4 (Sep 27) — The heart behind the song

Passages: 1 Samuel 16:1–13 (God looks at the heart) and Amos 5:21–24 (God rejects worship without heart/justice). Big idea: God cares more about the heart than the performance. He'll take an honest heart over a flawless one every time. Discuss: God tells Samuel, "people look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart" — how does that change how you think about being on a stage? · Amos is shocking: God says He hates their worship gatherings. Why? · Can you play a song perfectly and still not really worship?

Week 5 (Oct 4) — Why God is worth it (worth-ship)

Passage: Revelation 4–5 (the throne room; "Worthy is the Lamb"). Big idea: The old word "worth-ship" means declaring someone's worth. Worship is a response to who God actually is. Discuss: What are the beings around the throne actually doing? · If worship is declaring worth, what does the way you spend your time and money say you think is worth the most? · Why does heaven never seem to get bored of it?

Week 6 (Oct 11) — David: the wholehearted worshiper

Passage: 2 Samuel 6:12–23 (David dances before the Lord; Michal despises him). Big idea: Real worship can look undignified. David cared more about honoring God than about how he looked. Discuss: Michal was embarrassed by David — have you ever held back in worship because of what people might think? · David says he'll become "even more undignified than this." What do you make of that? · Where's the line between authentic expression and just performing?

Week 7 (Oct 18) — Worship when you don't feel it

Passage: Psalm 42–43 ("Why, my soul, are you downcast?"). Big idea: Worship isn't only for the good days. Sometimes it's talking to your own soul until your heart catches up. Discuss: The psalmist argues with himself — "put your hope in God." Is that fake, or is that faith? · How do you worship on a day you don't feel like it? · Why might God be honored more by worship that costs you something emotionally?

Week 8 (Oct 25) — The Bible's own songbook

Passages: Overview of the Psalms; read Psalm 100. Big idea: God gave His people 150 songs. They model honesty, range, and structure — the same things we'll learn about writing and arranging. Discuss: What's different about singing words God gave us versus words we came up with? · Psalm 100 is all celebration — but the Psalms also include anger and grief. Why include all of it? · What would it look like to make your own "psalm" this week? Tie-in: Pairs naturally with the musicianship topic on charts/arrangement — the Psalms have structure too.

Week 9 (Nov 1) — Worship costs something

Passages: 2 Samuel 24:18–25 ("I will not sacrifice… that costs me nothing") and Mark 12:41–44 (the widow's offering). Big idea: Worship that costs nothing isn't worth much. God notices what it costs you, not what it's worth to everyone else. Discuss: David refuses free stuff for his offering — why? · The widow gave two small coins; Jesus says she gave the most. How does God do the math differently than we do? · What does worship "cost" a musician — time, ego, comfort?

Week 10 (Nov 8) — Servant, not star

Passages: Philippians 2:1–11 (Christ's humility) and Colossians 3:23. Big idea: The musician on a worship team is a servant, not a star. We play for an audience of One and in service of the room. Discuss: Jesus "made himself nothing" — what does that model for someone with a gift and a microphone? · What's the difference between using your gift to serve and using the platform to be seen? · "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord" — how does that raise the bar on how we practice?

Week 11 (Nov 15) — Gratitude

Passages: Psalm 103 and 1 Thessalonians 5:16–18. Big idea: Thanksgiving is the native language of worship. "Give thanks in all circumstances" — not for everything, but in everything. Discuss: Psalm 103 lists what God has done — make your own list out loud. · Why is gratitude so hard to hold onto? · How could gratitude change the vibe of a rehearsal or a hard week? (Fits the week before Thanksgiving.)

(Nov 22 — Thanksgiving break, no meeting.)

Week 12 (Nov 29) — The first Christmas songs

Passage: Luke 1:46–55 (Mary's Magnificat); optionally the angels in Luke 2:13–14. Big idea: When God stepped into the world, people burst into song. Worship is the natural response to good news. Discuss: Mary's song is bold — God "brought down rulers" and "lifted up the humble." Does that match your picture of a Christmas song? · Why do you think Scripture keeps breaking into song at the big moments? · What's your honest response to the Christmas story this year? Tie-in: Kicks off the Christmas repertoire for the Showcase.

Week 13 (Dec 6) — Point away from yourself

Passages: Colossians 1:15–20 and John 3:30 ("He must become greater; I must become less"). Big idea: A worship leader's whole job is to point past themselves to Jesus. The best leaders make you forget about the leader. Discuss: John the Baptist was famous, and he said he wanted to decrease — why? · How do you lead people somewhere without making it about you? · What does it look like to "point to Jesus" with an instrument, not just words?

Week 14 (Dec 13) — Showcase & testimony

Passage: Light — Psalm 96:1–3 ("Sing to the Lord a new song… declare his glory among the nations"). Big idea: Celebrate the term. Play the songs. Share what God's been doing. Format: Shorter study, longer celebration. Invite each student to say one thing they've learned — musically or spiritually. Then play. (This is Showcase night — see the schedule.)

Winter Term — "The Worshiping Life"

Week 15 (Jan 3) — Reset: the worshiping life

Passage: Colossians 3:1–17 (set your hearts on things above; "whatever you do… do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus"). Big idea: A fresh start on the year. Worship is an orientation of the whole life, and it shows up in how we treat each other. Discuss: Paul lists things to "put off" and "put on" — which one is God putting a finger on for you this year? · What's one goal — musical or spiritual — you want to set for this term?

Week 16 (Jan 10) — The full range of worship

Passages: Psalm 13 (lament) and Psalm 150 (pure praise). Big idea: Worship holds the whole range of human emotion. God can handle your honesty. "How long, Lord?" is in the songbook too. Discuss: Psalm 13 starts angry and ends trusting — what changed? · Why is it healthy that the Bible's worship book includes complaints? · Which is easier for you to bring to God: praise or pain?

Week 17 (Jan 17) — Young and setting the example

Passage: 1 Timothy 4:12 ("Don't let anyone look down on you because you are young, but set an example…"). Big idea: Character on stage starts with character off stage. Who you are in private is who you really are. Discuss: Paul names five areas: speech, conduct, love, faith, purity. Which is hardest right now? · Why would being young make people underestimate you — and how do you answer that? · Does what you post/say/do off stage matter to how you lead on it?

Week 18 (Jan 24) — One body, many parts

Passage: 1 Corinthians 12:12–27 (many parts, one body). Big idea: A band is a living picture of the church — different roles, one sound, nobody unnecessary. Discuss: Paul says the parts that seem weaker are indispensable — how is that true in a band? · When have you wanted to be a different "part" than you are? · How do you celebrate a bandmate's moment instead of competing with it? Tie-in: Pairs with the musicianship topic on being a bandmate and the rhythm-section relationship.

Week 19 (Jan 31) — Chasing God's presence

Passages: Exodus 33:12–23 ("If your Presence does not go with us…") and Psalm 84. Big idea: The goal was never the music. The goal is God Himself. Moses would rather have God's presence than the promised land. Discuss: Moses turns down everything unless God comes too — do you want the gift or the Giver? · Psalm 84: "Better is one day in your courts than a thousand elsewhere." Do you believe that? · How do you keep the music from becoming the point?

Week 20 (Feb 7) — Use what you've been given

Passages: Matthew 25:14–30 (the talents) and 1 Peter 4:10–11. Big idea: God gives gifts to be invested, not buried. Using your gift is stewardship and worship. Discuss: The servant who buried his talent was afraid — what fears keep us from using our gifts? · "Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others" — who benefits when you develop your gift? · What's one way you could "invest" your musical gift this month?

Week 21 (Feb 14) — Worship as witness

Passages: Psalm 40:1–3 ("He put a new song in my mouth… many will see and fear") and Matthew 5:14–16. Big idea: A changed life set to music makes people curious about God. Worship spills outward. Discuss: In Psalm 40 the result of the new song is that others come to trust God — have you ever been drawn to God by someone else's joy? · "Let your light shine before others" — how is playing music a kind of light? · Where's your "stage" outside of church?

Week 22 (Feb 21) — The one thing

Passage: Psalm 27:4 ("One thing I ask… to gaze on the beauty of the Lord"). Big idea: Commissioning. Under all the skills and gear, the lifelong aim is simply to know and enjoy God. Look ahead to spring. Format: Reflect on the two terms, set individual next steps (a team to serve on, a skill to grow, a habit to keep), and pray over each student. Celebrate.

Notes for leaders
  • Keep it discussion, not lecture. The questions matter more than getting through the passage.
  • Model honesty. Leaders sharing a real struggle unlocks the room faster than anything.
  • Connect it forward. Where a study ties to the night's musicianship topic or song, name it out loud — students remember the theology when it's attached to their hands.
  • Alternate/backup studies if you need them: Psalm 63, Isaiah 6:1–8 (Isaiah's vision & "here am I, send me"), Hebrews 12:28–29, John 12:1–8 (Mary's extravagant worship), 1 Chronicles 16, Nehemiah 8.

Section 02 · the full arc

Musicianship topics

Format: ~20 minutes each week. Philosophy: teach the things nobody teaches you.

Anyone can learn the notes from YouTube. What separates a kid who can play at home from a musician a worship team actually wants is everything around the notes: how sound gets from your hands to the room, how a band communicates, how you show up prepared, and how a set comes together. That's this track. Every topic below pairs a short teach with a hands-on demo — because a DI box explained is forgettable, but a DI box you plugged in and heard the difference through is not.

Wherever possible the week's topic connects to that week's song and even the Bible study (structure in music ↔ structure in the Psalms; serving the song ↔ servant not star).

Fall Term — "The Ecosystem of Playing"

Week 1 (Sep 6) — What a band actually is

The roles on a worship team and how a Sunday really comes together: music director/band leader, the rhythm section (drums, bass, keys, rhythm guitar) as the engine, lead/electric, vocals and harmonies, and the tech team behind it all. Set expectations for the group: prepared, on time, humble, having fun. Demo: Walk the room to each instrument; have each leader play the same eight bars so students hear what each part contributes to one groove.

Week 2 (Sep 13) — How sound gets to the room (signal flow)

The single most useful mental model in live music: instrument → (DI box or amp/mic) → stage box/snake → console → PA speakers → the room; and a parallel path to monitors/in-ears. Once you can picture the chain, you can troubleshoot almost anything ("where in the chain did it break?"). Demo: Trace one real signal path live — plug a bass or acoustic through a DI to the console and literally point at each stage as the sound travels.

Week 3 (Sep 20) — Cables, connections & clean power

XLR vs. 1/4" (TS vs. TRS), why "balanced" signals reject noise on long runs, what a DI box does and when you need one, and clean/consistent power — why we don't daisy-chain into a random wall outlet and why gear likes stable power. Cable care (the over-under wrap) so gear lasts. Demo: Pass around an XLR, a TS instrument cable, and a DI. Do the "hum" demo: a long unbalanced run vs. a balanced run so they hear why XLR exists. Teach the over-under cable wrap and have everyone try it.

Week 4 (Sep 27) — In-ear monitors & stage sound

Why modern stages are quiet: in-ears vs. floor wedges, what a personal monitor mix is ("more me"), how to build a usable mix (start with click + your own instrument, add what you need to lock in), and — non-negotiable — protecting your hearing. Demo: Let students listen to a simple in-ear mix and add/remove instruments so they hear what "building a mix" means. Talk decibels and why IEMs at a sane volume protect their ears for a lifetime.

Week 5 (Oct 4) — Tuning, tone & gain staging

Tuning to a shared reference and why an out-of-tune instrument poisons the whole band; the basics of gain staging (set levels so nothing clips and nothing's buried); and a first pass at instrument-appropriate tone (bass: clean and present; keys: the right patch; guitar: not too much gain). Demo: Tune every instrument together to the same reference. Then intentionally set a bad gain (too hot, then too quiet) and a good one so they hear the difference.

Week 6 (Oct 11) — The click and the loop

Playing to a metronome/click track; why the click is the band's shared heartbeat; an intro to tracks and pads (the ambient bed a lot of modern worship uses) and how they lock a band together. Timing is the one skill every player is judged on. Demo: Everyone plays a simple groove without a click (hear it drift), then with a click (hear it lock). Let a student conduct the band starting and stopping on the count.

Week 7 (Oct 18) — Rehearsing at home

How to actually practice so rehearsal isn't where you learn the song: listen to the recording on repeat, learn from a chart, slow the hard part down and loop it, practice to the track, and mark your own roadmap. Come to band rehearsal already knowing your part — rehearsal is for playing together, not learning notes. Demo: Take that week's song and model a 10-minute home-practice session out loud — including looping one tricky bar and playing along with the recording.

Week 8 (Oct 25) — Charts & the number system

Reading a chord chart, song structure/roadmap markings (intro, V, PC, C, B, tag), and the Nashville Number System so you can transpose a song to any key on the fly — the single skill that makes you a "call any key" musician. Demo: Put that week's song on a chart, then rewrite the chorus in numbers and transpose it to a new key together in real time.

Week 9 (Nov 1) — Rehearsing as a band

What a good rehearsal looks like: start on time, run for arrangement first then details, listen more than you play, and lock the rhythm section (bass + kick drum are married). Dynamics as a team decision, not five soloists. Demo: Run that week's song twice — first everyone plays full the whole time (muddy), then with dynamics and space. Let them hear how "playing less" sounds like more.

Week 10 (Nov 8) — The music director

What an MD/band leader does before and during a service: sets the arrangement and keys, counts things off, and cues the band in real time. Onstage communication — hand signals, eye contact, verbal cues on the mic — and the skill of following a leader. Demo: Teach a few standard cues (build, drop/soft, one more time, tag, cutoff) and have a student "MD" the band through a song using only those signals.

Week 11 (Nov 15) — Dynamics & arrangement

Building a song like a story: intro, verses, pre-chorus lift, chorus, breakdown, bridge, big moment, tag. Leaving space; playing for the song, not for yourself; how the same three chords feel different at each stage. Demo: Take one chorus and arrange it three ways — full band, just pad + vocal, and building back up — so students feel how arrangement creates emotion.

Week 12 (Nov 29) — Planning a set & picking songs

How a set is built: flow and momentum, keys that transition well, matching songs to a theme or moment, and why song order matters. This is the on-ramp to Planning Center Services. Demo: As a group, build a 3-song set from the songs learned so far — argue about order and keys, then play the transitions.

Week 13 (Dec 6) — The production world

The people and gear that make a service happen beyond the band: front-of-house sound, monitors, lighting, running lyrics/ProPresenter, cameras/stream, and the stage plot & input list that tell everyone where things go. Respecting and communicating with the tech team. Demo: Draw the group's own stage plot and input list on a whiteboard — where each player stands, what plugs in where — the same document a real production team works from.

Week 14 (Dec 13) — Soundcheck & line check (Showcase)

Putting it all together for the Showcase: how to run a line check (confirm every input one at a time), a soundcheck (get levels and monitor mixes), and settle nerves before you play. Then perform. Demo: Run a real line check and soundcheck for the Showcase set. This is the lesson.

Winter Term — "Becoming a Working Band"

Week 15 (Jan 3) — Gear deep-dive I: strings & keys

Guitar and bass signal chains — amps vs. DI, the basics of effects pedals (tuner, overdrive, delay, reverb) and pedal order; keys and pads — patches, splits/layers, and the sound that glues a band. What gear actually earns a spot on your board and what's just GAS (gear acquisition syndrome). Demo: Run a guitar or bass through a couple of pedals and change the order live; show the same keyboard part on three different patches.

Week 16 (Jan 10) — Gear deep-dive II: drums & vocals

Drums — tuning and muffling, acoustic vs. electronic kits, playing to tracks/triggers, and the kick-snare backbone; vocals — mic technique (distance, proximity, not eating the mic), monitors for singers, and blend/harmony basics. Demo: Tune a snare high and low so they hear the difference; do a quick mic-distance demo on a vocalist (too far, too close, right).

Week 17 (Jan 17) — Communication & tools

How real teams communicate and stay organized: Planning Center (schedules, plans, the song's chart/MP3/key all in one place), group chats, and shared charts. Rehearsal etiquette and the underrated skill of being reliable — replying, showing up, being prepared. Demo: Walk through a real Planning Center Services plan for a Sunday — show how a scheduled player sees their music, their key, and their spot in the order.

Week 18 (Jan 24) — Being a bandmate

The rhythm-section relationship (bass locks to the kick drum), feel and pocket, listening as a skill, and serving the song and each other. The difference between a group of players and an actual band. Demo: Isolate just bass + drums on a groove and dial in the lock; then add the rest of the band on top so they hear the foundation.

Week 19 (Jan 31) — Leading vs. playing

What changes when you're the worship leader, not just a player: engaging and leading a congregation, singing/talking on mic, holding the room, and the servant-leadership posture behind it. Demo: Have a student lead the band and "the congregation" (the rest of the group) through one song, including a spoken cue or transition.

Week 20 (Feb 7) — Building & running a full set

From single songs to a connected set: planning a multi-song run, transitions and segues, key relationships, and keeping momentum across a whole set instead of stopping between every song. Demo: Build and run a full 3–4 song set with real transitions — no stopping between songs.

Week 21 (Feb 14) — Record it & review it

Capturing a rehearsal on a phone or interface, then listening back critically but kindly — timing, dynamics, blend — and how to give and receive feedback without egos. Self-review is how pros improve fastest. Demo: Record the band playing one song, then listen back together and each name one thing to keep and one thing to fix.

Week 22 (Feb 21) — Put it together & what's next

A full set run as the "final," a review of how far the band's come, and a real conversation about next steps: serving on a North Metro team, growing a specific skill, or where to plug in this spring. Demo: Full set performance for each other; then map each student's next step.

Running list of "life of a musician" topics to weave in anytime

Beyond the scheduled topics, keep pulling from these as they come up naturally: how to accept a gig/serving slot professionally, showing up early and tearing down last, stage presence and what to do with your face, dealing with mistakes mid-song (keep going — the click doesn't stop), protecting your hearing for a 40-year career, taking care of your gear and other people's gear, being coachable, the business side (session work, being dependable = getting called back), and the spiritual discipline of not letting skill outrun character.

Making it run

Setup, promotion & logistics

The operational side — how we sign people up, spread the word, and cover the practical bases. Expand any section for the full detail.

Registrations is the front door, People is the roster, Groups is the week-to-week home base, and Services is both the teaching tool and the real thing we’re preparing them for.

Planning Center setup — the full guide

Written knowing you work at Planning Center — so this is about how to wire the products together for this specific group, not a tutorial on the buttons.

The clean mental model: Registrations is the front door (signup + payment + consent), People is the system of record (the roster and the parent/household links), Groups is the week-to-week home base (attendance, messaging, resources), and Services is the teaching tool and the real thing you're preparing them for (song library, plans, scheduling). You can run the whole program on these four.

Registrations — the front door

Use a Registration as the signup so you capture consent and (if you want) a materials fee in one flow.

  • Signup type: an event/class-style registration open through early September, with a soft cap that matches your room and leader ratio.
  • Attendee vs. account holder: the student is the attendee; the parent/guardian is the account holder. This matters for minors and keeps billing/communication on the adult.
  • Form fields to collect: student name, grade/age, primary instrument (+ secondary), rough experience level, whether they own/have access to an instrument, allergies/medical notes, and a photo/video media release (for the Showcase and promo). Add an emergency contact and a "how'd you hear about it" for future promotion.
  • Consent & waiver: attach your church's standard minor participation + media release as a required agreement.
  • Fee (optional): if there's a materials fee, collect it here; otherwise make it free and just use the form.
  • Attendee cap & waitlist: turn the waitlist on so a strong response becomes next-term's roster rather than a lost lead.

Registrations pushes attendees into People automatically, which is why it's the front door and not a standalone form.

People — the system of record

  • Workflow: build a "Worship Music School – Onboarding" Workflow so every new registrant gets the same steps: welcome email → confirm instrument/experience → add to the Group → parent added to the parent list.
  • Lists: maintain a Students list and a Parents/Guardians list (via household relationships) so you can message each audience separately.
  • Households: make sure each student is linked to a parent household — this is what lets you email parents and keep minors' communication routed through an adult.
  • Field data: store instrument, experience tier (Beginner/Intermediate/Advanced), and Showcase media-consent as custom fields so you can filter (e.g., "all intermediate bassists").
  • Background checks: confirm all three leaders (and any helpers) are cleared per the church's child-safety policy before Day 1.

Groups — the week-to-week home base

This is where the group actually lives between meetings.

  • One Group: "Worship Music School (Sun 2026–27)."
  • Attendance: take attendance every week — it's your simplest health metric and helps you follow up with a student who's drifting.
  • Messaging: use Group messaging for students and a parent-facing thread (or route parent comms through the Parents list in People). Weekly "here's what we did / here's what to practice" notes go here.
  • Resources tab: attach charts, chord sheets, reference tracks/playlists, and the song-of-the-week so students can practice at home. This is the beginning of the "download songs week to week" idea you mentioned — Groups Resources is the no-code version of that until a website exists.
  • Events: schedule the weekly meeting as a recurring Group event so attendance and reminders are automatic; add the Showcase as a special event parents can be invited to.

Services — the teaching tool and the destination

Services does double duty: it's how you show students how real set-building and scheduling works, and it's where your teaching repertoire lives.

  • Service Type: create a "Worship Music School" Service Type separate from your Sunday service types so you don't clutter production.
  • Song library: add each song from the Song Library into Services with its key, BPM, and attach the chart, chord chart, and a reference MP3/link. Now "learn this week's song" = "open the plan in Services," exactly like the real teams do.
  • Plans = your lessons: build a Plan per meeting (or per unit). Put the song(s) in order, set the key you're teaching, and add the arrangement. When you teach "planning a set" (musicianship Week 12) and "building a full set" (Week 20), you're literally doing it in Services in front of them.
  • Scheduling the kids: as students get ready, schedule them onto a Plan by position (drums, bass, keys, etc.). They get the accept/decline flow and see their music — the exact experience of being on a real team. This makes Week 17's "communication & tools" lesson concrete.
  • On-ramp to serving: when a student is ready to serve on an actual North Metro team, they're already fluent in the tool.

How it all connects (the flow)

  1. Parent signs the student up in Registrations (consent + info captured).
  2. Registrant lands in People; the Onboarding Workflow runs; student joins the Group, parent joins the Parents list.
  3. Week to week, Groups handles attendance, messaging, and practice resources.
  4. Services holds the song library and the lesson plans; as students progress you schedule them onto plans so they learn the real workflow.
  5. The Showcase is a Group event (for invites) backed by a Services plan (for the set).

A few setup decisions to make

  • Free vs. materials fee (drives whether Registrations needs payment on).
  • One Group vs. two (split if you end up with two bands).
  • Whether to use a dedicated Services Type now or reuse an existing one (dedicated is cleaner).
  • Who's the "admin" of record on each product so it survives if a leader steps back.

Keep it to three short slides. A QR code straight to the Registrations link beats a typed URL every time.

5. FAQ (for the email, a card, or a landing section)

Does my child need to already play well? No. We build tiered parts so a beginner and an experienced player can be in the same band and both have something to play.

What instruments? Drums, bass, guitar, keys/piano, and vocals are the core band. If they play something else, talk to us — we'll find a place.

Do they need their own gear? Ideally they bring their own instrument if they have one. We'll have some shared backline; tell us on the signup if access to an instrument is a barrier.

Is this a performance group or a class? Both, on purpose. It's discipleship and learning first, and it builds toward playing — including an end-of-term Showcase families are invited to.

Is it just worship music? Mostly, but we mix in the occasional secular song for range and fun.

How much of a commitment is it? Weekly, [time]. Consistency matters because it's a band — but life happens, just keep us posted.

6. Ongoing weekly communication (the cadence that keeps it alive)

The single habit that makes a group like this thrive is a short, reliable weekly note. Keep it to the same rhythm every week so students and parents know it's coming.

After each meeting — to students (via Groups):

Tonight we [studied ___ / covered ___ / worked on ___]. This week, practice: [song + the one thing to focus on]. Reference track + chart are in the Resources tab. See you Sunday. 🎶

Roughly monthly — to parents: A few sentences on what the group's been learning (spiritually and musically), a heads-up on anything coming (the Showcase, a break week), and one genuine encouragement about their student. Parents who feel looped in become your biggest advocates.

Before the Showcase — to everyone: Date, time, location, "invite your people," and what to expect. Make it feel like the event it is.

Promotion timeline (works backward from Sept 6)

  • ~4 weeks out (mid-Aug): signup opens; first parent email; slides begin looping.
  • ~3 weeks out: first on-stage announcement; student-channel push.
  • ~2 weeks out: second email with the FAQ; personal invites from leaders to specific kids (this converts best).
  • ~1 week out: "last call" announcement + reminder to registered families with the practical details.
  • Launch week: confirmation/welcome to registered students with room, time, and what to bring.

Logistics, safety &amp; the didn&rsquo;t-think-of-that list

This is the "everything else" file — the operational stuff that makes or breaks a group, the things worth deciding before Day 1, and a running list of ideas and open questions.

Room & space

You need a room that can hold a small band setup plus a circle for discussion, ideally with the flexibility to do both in one night. Look for: enough power (dedicated circuits are ideal so a full band doesn't trip a breaker), the ability to be a little loud without disrupting others, a screen or wall for projecting lyrics/charts, and storage or easy load-in for gear. Setting up a quick "discussion circle" area separate from the "band" area helps the three sections feel distinct.

Gear & tech — what you actually need

You can start lean and grow. A workable minimum:

  • Backline: a drum kit (or an electronic kit — great for volume control), a keyboard/stage piano with a stand and sustain pedal, one or two combo amps (guitar/bass) or DIs into the PA, and a bass.
  • PA / monitoring: a small powered PA or mixer, a couple of speakers, and at minimum one or two monitors — or, better for the in-ears lesson, a simple personal-monitor/IEM solution even if it's modest.
  • Mics & cables: a couple of vocal mics, a stock of XLR and instrument cables, DI boxes, and a power conditioner/quality power strips.
  • Consumables: drumsticks, a few sets of strings (guitar/bass), guitar picks, batteries (9V for pedals/active basses, AA), and disposable/individual in-ear tips for hygiene.
  • Tech: a laptop/tablet for lyrics-and-charts (ProPresenter or similar) and a metronome/click source; a phone or small interface for the "record and review" week.

Some of this may already exist in a youth room or can be borrowed from the main stage midweek. Flag on the signup whether students can bring their own instruments — that reduces what you need to provide.

Starter budget sketch (adjust to reality)

Think in three buckets: one-time (any PA/monitor/kit gaps, cables, DIs, mic stands), per-term consumables (strings, sticks, batteries, in-ear tips), and curriculum/printing (charts, any study materials). Even a modest recurring consumables line prevents the "nobody has a 9V battery" moment from derailing a rehearsal. Bring a specific number to the church once you've inventoried what's already available.

Child safety & protection

This is non-negotiable and should be settled before launch: - All leaders and any helpers background-checked and cleared per North Metro's policy. - Two-adult rule — never one adult alone with a student; keep the room visible/open. - Media release collected at signup for any Showcase photos/video or promo. - Check-in/pickup expectations clear to parents, especially for younger students. - Emergency contacts and medical/allergy notes captured at signup and accessible to leaders. - Communication with minors routed through parents/an approved channel, per church policy — not private DMs.

Leaders & ratios

Three leaders is a strong core (rhythm section + theory/strings + vocals). Watch your student-to-leader ratio as it grows; if you push past a comfortable band size, that's the signal to recruit a helper or split into two bands rather than dilute the experience. Define now who owns what: overall direction and rhythm section (Evan), instrument/theory instruction (co-leader), vocals and leading (vocalist), and who owns the Planning Center admin and parent communication.

Home-practice expectations (a simple student covenant)

Set the tone Day 1 with a short, positive agreement students opt into — something like: I'll come prepared (I'll practice my part at home), I'll be on time and present, I'll serve the song and my bandmates, I'll take care of the gear, and I'll bring a teachable, encouraging attitude. Keep it aspirational, not punitive. Reinforce that "rehearsal is for playing together, not learning the notes" — that one expectation, held kindly, changes everything.

Attendance & follow-up

Take attendance every week in Groups. It's your earliest warning system: two missed weeks from a kid who was engaged is a text-and-check-in, not a discipline issue. The relationship is the retention strategy.

The Showcase (Week 14 / Dec 13)

Treat it as a real event, because it's the payoff that makes everything feel worth it. Decide the format early: a standalone family showcase, or folding the band into a youth or weekend service. Handle invites through the Group event, run a real soundcheck (that's literally the week's lesson), keep it short and celebratory, and make space for a student or two to share what they've learned. Photos/video (with the release on file) become next term's promotion.

Contingencies

  • Too many signups: cap via Registrations and run a waitlist; a big response is a two-band problem, which is a good problem — recruit one more helper.
  • Too few: run it anyway with whoever comes; a tight small band is a great first term, and word-of-mouth after the Showcase fills term two.
  • Very mixed levels: lean on the tiered song parts (Song Library difficulty ratings), pair a stronger student with a newer one, and let advanced players push ahead on the "finish" weeks while beginners cement the basics.
  • A leader is out a given week: the three-leader structure means you can cover; keep plans in Planning Center so anyone can run the night.

Things you may not have thought of (a starter list)

  • A consistent name and simple brand (a logo, a color) makes promotion and a future website far easier — worth locking early.
  • A private playlist (Spotify/Apple) of the term's songs so students marinate in the repertoire all week.
  • Photo/video capture most weeks (with releases) — it fuels promotion and gives students something to be proud of.
  • A guest week — bring in a working musician, a FOH engineer, or a lighting tech for a Q&A; it makes the "production world" lesson unforgettable.
  • Instrument access equity — quietly make sure a kid without gear at home isn't left out (loaner instrument, practice time in the room).
  • A "serving on-ramp" — a clear, celebrated path from the group onto a real North Metro team for students who are ready.
  • Parent buy-in touchpoints — the monthly parent note and the Showcase invite turn parents into advocates and drivers-to-practice.
  • Hearing protection framed as a career skill, not a rule — it lands better and it's true.
  • A term-end feedback loop — a two-minute survey to students/parents to improve the next term.
  • The future website you mentioned — the natural v1 is just the Groups Resources tab (charts + reference tracks per week); a real site can come later once the content pattern is proven.
